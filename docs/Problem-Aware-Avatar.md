# Shortlist — Problem Aware Avatar

> **Offer context (assumptions):** *Shortlist* is an AI-powered shopping-decision app that curates and narrows overwhelming option sets into a confident shortlist of three. Audience: time-strapped working professionals who lose hours to research, comparison, and second-guessing. Freemium, ~$9.99/mo premium. Benefit: reclaim time and decide faster without regret.

**Stage of Awareness:** Problem Aware — she feels the *symptom* (lost time, decision exhaustion, purchase regret) but does not yet know a product category exists to solve it. She is not searching "shopping assistant app." She blames the volume of options, the internet, or herself.

---

- **Name:** Danielle Reyes
- **Gender:** Female
- **Job:** Senior Marketing Manager at a mid-size SaaS company (manages a team of 6; hybrid, 3 days in-office)
- **Household Income:** $185,000 combined ($118K her salary; spouse contributes the remainder)
- **Marital Status:** Married, two children (ages 7 and 10)
- **Education Level:** B.A. in Communications; halfway through a part-time MBA

## B → What They Do & Like

**Top 3 Brands they wear:** Madewell, Athleta, Allbirds — trusted defaults that remove decision friction.

**1–2 Hobbies they have:** Peloton/running (scheduled like a meeting); weekend cooking from a rotation of ~8 trusted recipes so she never has to "decide" dinner.

**Top 5 Favorite movies:** *The Devil Wears Prada*, *Hidden Figures*, *Julie & Julia*, *Knives Out*, *Crazy Rich Asians* — competence-under-pressure narratives.

**Top 5 Favorite books:** *Atomic Habits* (James Clear), *Essentialism* (Greg McKeown), *The Paradox of Choice* (Barry Schwartz — she read it and felt seen but never sought a tool), *Big Magic* (Elizabeth Gilbert), *Where the Crawdads Sing*.

**Top 5 visited websites:** Amazon, Google, LinkedIn, The Wirecutter (NYT), Reddit (r/BuyItForLife, r/Frugal).

**Top 5 relevant social media influencers:** "Organized home" creators on Instagram, @thefinancialdiet, Wirecutter-style review accounts, "honest mom" creators, budgeting/productivity TikTokers (the "deinfluencing" wave).

## C → Why Are They

**Main Personality Traits:** Conscientious, optimizing, mildly perfectionist, time-anxious, quietly competitive. A **maximizer** — the cohort most damaged by choice overload, prone to regret and post-purchase doubt.

**5 Major Values They Hold:** Efficiency, competence, family security, fairness/value-for-money, personal growth.

**2 Major Life Victories:** (1) Promotion to senior manager while pregnant with her second child. (2) Buying their first home after a disciplined two-year savings plan.

**2 Major Life Failures:** (1) Burned out hard in 2022 and took leave — she privately fears repeating it. (2) A "research-paralysis" stroller purchase: 14 hours of comparison, bought the wrong one anyway, still resents it.

## D → Smart Market Questions

**What keeps them awake at night, eyes open, staring at the ceiling:** The feeling that she is *busy but not in control* — that her time is being eaten in 20-minute increments she can't account for, and that she's modeling "frazzled" for her kids.

**What are they secretly afraid of in life:** That she's mediocre at everything because she's spread too thin — and that the constant low-grade decision fatigue means she's failing to "have it together" the way she appears to.

**What are they angry about, and who are they angry at:** Angry at *the volume* — at every brand, retailer, and feed that turns a simple purchase into a 40-tab project. Angry at herself for "not just deciding," and at an internet that turns choosing into exhausting cognitive labor.

**Top 3 frustrations they feel every day:**
1. Opening an app to buy *one* thing and losing 30 minutes to comparison.
2. Reading 30 contradictory reviews and trusting none.
3. Re-researching the *same* category months later because she never captured the decision.

**Biggest secret desire in life:** To feel *unhurried* — to have decisions, especially small ones, simply handled so her attention is reserved for things that matter.

**Built-in bias to how they make decisions:** Defaults to trusted authorities (Wirecutter, a friend's text) to short-circuit analysis; heavily loss-averse — fears the *wrong* choice more than she wants the *best* one.

**Common words or language unique to them:** "Rabbit hole," "analysis paralysis," "I don't have the bandwidth," "just tell me what to buy," "too many tabs," "I'll deal with it later."

**Top 3 complaints about existing solutions:** (1) Review sites feel gamed/sponsored. (2) Marketplace search surfaces volume, not a decision. (3) Asking friends is slow and inconsistent.

## E → Going Deep

**Top 3 Dominant Negative Emotions:** Overwhelm, self-directed guilt, simmering resentment toward the shopping process.

**Top 3 Dominant Positive Emotions From Solving This Problem:** Relief, regained control/competence, the lightness of reclaimed time.

**Top 3 Beliefs They Hold About The World:** (1) Time is the only truly scarce resource. (2) More options have made life worse, not better. (3) The "right" answer exists — she just can't find it efficiently.

**Biggest Lifestyle Desire:** A calmer, more intentional life where her energy goes to family, career, and growth — not logistics.

## F → Purchasing Habits

**Top 3 Decision Triggers:** A trusted recommendation that removes risk; a credible time-savings claim; social proof from people like her.

**Prior Purchases For This Pain:** Productivity apps (Notion, Todoist), grocery/meal-kit delivery, *Essentialism*-style books, a budgeting app — all adjacent buys aimed at reclaiming time, none aimed at shopping decisions specifically. **This is the tell of Problem Awareness:** she's bought *around* the problem without naming the category.

**Price Tolerance For Offer:** High receptivity to ~$9.99/mo *if* time-savings is concrete; she already spends more on time-saving services. Free trial likely mandatory given category novelty.

**Time Horizon Of Solution:** Wants relief immediately; judges value within the first 1–2 uses.

## G → Primary Wants

- **Wants to gain:** Time, confidence in her choices, mental bandwidth.
- **Wants to be:** Someone who has it together — decisive, calm, in control.
- **Wants to do:** Make good purchases in minutes and move on.
- **Wants to save:** Hours per week, and the cognitive cost of regret.
- **Wants to avoid:** Buyer's remorse, "rabbit holes," wasting a Saturday on a $60 decision.

## H → Empathy Map

- **Seeing:** Endless product grids, infinite scroll, conflicting star ratings, sponsored results, a browser with 22 open tabs.
- **Thinking:** *"Why is this so hard? I just want the right one. I don't have time for this."*
- **Hearing:** Coworkers and friends saying "just get this one" with no real basis; ads promising the "best."
- **Feeling:** Drained, behind, mildly ashamed that something this small derails her.
- **Saying:** "I'll research it this weekend." "I can't decide." "There are too many options."
- **Doing:** Bookmarking, abandoning carts, deferring, then buying out of exhaustion.

---

## Strategic implication

Because Danielle is **Problem Aware**, lead with the symptom, not the app. Mirror the pain ("Lost another Saturday to 40 tabs and still bought the wrong thing?") before introducing that a solution category even exists. Naming the product too early skips the awareness step and lowers conversion. Trend tailwind: choice overload, infinite-scroll fatigue, and the "deinfluencing" backlash all signal a market actively *feeling* this problem in 2025–26, even if it hasn't yet gone looking for a fix.

**Grounding (category research, not Shortlist's own performance):** a meta-analysis of ~100 studies found excessive options reduce satisfaction, increase regret, and lower the likelihood of choosing at all; Meta has reported ~42% of users abandon online purchases due to cognitive overload; Baymard puts cart abandonment at ~69.6%.
