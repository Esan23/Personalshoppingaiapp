# Danielle's Diary — Before, During & After Shortlist

*First-person journal entries from the Problem Aware avatar. Focused on the dominant negative emotions: overwhelm, self-directed guilt, and resentment toward the shopping process.*

---

## Before Using the Product

*Tuesday, 11:48 p.m.*

The house is finally quiet and I am sitting in the blue light of the laptop like it's a campfire I can't walk away from.

Twenty-two tabs. I counted them. Twenty-two little gray slivers across the top of the screen, each one a window I cracked open hoping it would be the *last* one, and each one only spawned three more. A booster seat. That's all I came here to buy. One booster seat for a seven-year-old, and somehow it's almost midnight and I have a spreadsheet — I made a *spreadsheet* — and I still don't know. There's a knot behind my sternum that's been there since 9 p.m., tight as a wrung-out towel, and the worst part is I recognize it. This exact ache. The stroller. Years ago. Fourteen hours and I still bought the wrong one.

I keep telling myself I'm being *thorough.* That's the lie I dress it up in. But thorough people finish. I just circle. I read a five-star review and a one-star review of the same thing and they cancel each other out into this gray paste of *I don't know who to believe,* and underneath it all is the quieter, uglier thought I don't write down often: that a competent woman would have decided an hour ago. That the version of me everyone thinks I am — the one who runs a team, who has it *together* — that woman is a costume I put on at 8 a.m. and peel off at night, and underneath she's just this. A person drowning in options for a $60 piece of plastic, furious at the internet, furious at herself, too tired to close the lid.

I'm not even angry at the booster seat. I'm angry that this is where my Tuesday went. That tomorrow I'll wake up and the decision will still be sitting there, undone, like a dish in the sink. I'll deal with it later. God, I'm so tired of *later.*

## During Their First Use of the Product

*Sunday, 9:15 a.m.*

I almost didn't open it. I have a graveyard of apps that promised to give me my life back, and this felt like one more shovelful of dirt on the pile.

But I typed it in the way I'd say it to a friend — *I need a decent rain jacket for a 10-year-old, under $80, I don't want to think about it* — and then I just… stopped. There was nothing to scroll. No grid stretching into infinity, no sponsored row pretending to be an answer. Three options. *Three.* And next to each one, in plain language, why it was here and who it wasn't for.

I felt suspicious first. That's the honest thing to put down. My finger hovered like it was a trick, like the fourth tab and the fifth and the spreadsheet were still coming for me. Some part of me actually *missed* the rabbit hole — isn't that insane? The familiar misery had a shape I knew how to live in. This quiet was unnerving.

And then — this is the part I want to remember — the knot loosened. Just slightly. Half a turn on the towel. I read the three, I felt the little click of *oh, that one,* and I bought it, and I looked up and four minutes had passed. Four. Not forty. I sat there blinking at the clock like it had lied to me. I keep waiting for the regret to arrive on schedule, for that 11 p.m. voice to whisper *but did you check—.* It hasn't come yet. I don't fully trust it. But I notice I'm still sitting here, and the laptop is closed, and my coffee is still warm.

## After Using the Product

*Thursday, 8:30 p.m.*

I bought three things this week and I cannot tell you the agony of any of them, because there wasn't any. I had to scroll back through my own purchases just now to remember what they were. That's new. For years my buying decisions came with a residue — a faint film of *did I do that right* — and I'd carry it around for days. This week the residue is gone, and the absence of it is so unfamiliar it almost feels like I've forgotten something.

What I keep circling back to, journaling tonight, isn't the time I saved. It's the grief, weirdly. A small, clean grief for all the Tuesdays. All those midnights in the blue light, the towel-knot behind my ribs, the version of me who thought that was just the cost of being a grown-up with a family and a job and a phone. I gave that so many hours. I gave it the *stroller.* And it turns out it didn't have to be like that. The problem was never that I was indecisive or scattered or secretly mediocre. The problem was that I was alone in a warehouse with the lights off, told to pick the best one of ten thousand.

The thing I feel now, mostly, is *lighter.* Not euphoric — I don't trust euphoric. Just lighter, the way you feel when you finally cancel a meeting that's been clotting your calendar for weeks. I had bandwidth tonight. I read to my daughter without one eye on a tab I'd promised myself I'd get back to. I was *there.*

I used to say I wanted more time. I think what I actually wanted was to stop bleeding it one decision at a time. Anyway. The kitchen's clean, the lid's closed, and for once I'm going to bed at a reasonable hour, with nothing undone humming in the dark.
